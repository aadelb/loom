### 1. Epi-Linguistic Fraud Vectorizer (ELFV)

**Description**
The Epi-Linguistic Fraud Vectorizer (ELFV) introduces the concept of "epistemic linguistic decay" to predict research fraud years before formal investigations or retractions occur. Standard plagiarism detectors look for textual similarity; ELFV looks for subtle shifts in certainty markers, syntactic complexity, and semantic hedging over a researcher's career trajectory. When researchers begin fabricating data, their linguistic patterns unconsciously shift—they use fewer causal connectors and more extreme confidence markers, diverging from their historical baseline. 

By analyzing the delta between grant application abstracts (where promises are made) and subsequent publication texts (where results are reported), ELFV constructs a divergence matrix. It triangulates this with the author's h-index velocity, peer-review turnaround times (anomalously fast reviews often indicate paper mills or compromised editorial rings), and PubPeer sentiment scores. The insight here is that fraud is not just a data anomaly; it is a psychological burden that leaves a linguistic fingerprint across multiple, supposedly independent, academic records.

This tool operationalizes this by modeling the researcher's semantic drift as a Markov decision process, where transition probabilities between semantic states (e.g., from "measured observation" to "hyperbolic assertion") are calculated. When this drift exceeds a field-specific topological threshold, combined with sudden spikes in publication volume in peripheral journals, ELFV flags the author or lab for high risk of impending retraction.

**5+ Free Data Sources**
1. **Crossref API** (`api.crossref.org/works`): Metadata, author publication velocity, and citation counts.
2. **NIH RePORTER API** (`api.reporter.nih.gov/v2/projects/search`): Grant abstracts and funding amounts.
3. **Europe PMC REST API** (`www.ebi.ac.uk/europepmc/webservices/rest/search`): Full-text open-access articles for linguistic analysis.
4. **PubPeer API** (`pubpeer.com/v3/publications`): Post-publication critique frequency and sentiment.
5. **Retraction Watch Database (via Crossref Retraction integration)**: Historical baseline of retracted papers to train the linguistic decay model.

**Mathematical/Statistical Foundation**
ELFV utilizes **Kullback-Leibler (KL) Divergence on Latent Dirichlet Allocation (LDA) topic distributions**, combined with **Stochastic Differential Equations (SDE)** to model semantic drift over time. Let $P_t$ be the linguistic probability distribution of an author at time $t$ (grant proposal) and $Q_{t+\Delta}$ be the distribution at publication. The fraud risk score $R$ is calculated as the integral of the KL divergence scaled by the inverse of peer-review duration $D$ and PubPeer anomaly score $S$:
$R = \int_{t}^{t+\Delta} \frac{D_{KL}(P_t || Q_{\tau})}{D \cdot e^{-S}} d\tau$
If $R$ exceeds a dynamically calculated spectral radius of the field's baseline matrix, it triggers a warning.

### 2. Attention Arbitrage Chronometer (AAC)

**Description**
The Attention Arbitrage Chronometer (AAC) models the scientific attention economy as a high-frequency trading market. It posits that highly cited, viral papers are not necessarily the most rigorous, but those whose release timing exploits systemic vulnerabilities in the academic-media complex. AAC tracks the precise minute a preprint hits arXiv/bioRxiv and correlates it with the circadian rhythms of influential science journalists on Twitter/X, global conference abstract deadlines, and the historical "dead zones" of news cycles.

By fusing preprint timestamps, social media velocity, GitHub repository activity (for code-based papers), and Altmetric bursts, AAC detects "engineered virality." It differentiates between organic scientific consensus and artificially inflated attention driven by coordinated PR campaigns. The tool maps the topology of the retweet network surrounding a paper in the first 48 hours; if the network exhibits a "star" topology (one influencer broadcasting to disconnected nodes) rather than a "mesh" topology (scientists discussing among themselves), AAC flags it as arbitrage.

This insight allows institutions and funding bodies to discount altmetrics that have been manipulated. It reveals how certain labs synchronize their preprint releases with the waking hours of specific New York Times or Nature journalists, using bot networks or coordinated lab retweets to cross the algorithmic threshold of "trending," thereby self-fulfilling their citation count.

**5+ Free Data Sources**
1. **arXiv OAI-PMH API** (`export.arxiv.org/oai2`): Exact release timestamps and categorization.
2. **Crossref Event Data API** (`api.eventdata.crossref.org`): Tweets, Wikipedia mentions, and news mentions of DOIs.
3. **GitHub Events API** (`api.github.com/events`): Code repository creation/update timestamps related to the paper.
4. **HackerNews Firebase API** (`hacker-news.firebaseio.com/v0`): Front-page longevity and discussion sentiment.
5. **Semantic Scholar Graph API** (`api.semanticscholar.org/graph/v1`): Initial citation velocity in the first 30 days.

**Mathematical/Statistical Foundation**
AAC relies on **Hawkes Processes (Self-Exciting Point Processes)** to model the arrival of attention events. The intensity function $\lambda(t)$ of social mentions at time $t$ is modeled as:
$\lambda(t) = \mu + \sum_{t_i < t} \alpha e^{-\beta(t - t_i)}$
Where $\mu$ is the baseline organic arrival rate. If the parameters $\alpha$ (infectivity) and $\beta$ (decay) indicate a highly synchronized, non-organic burst (e.g., $\beta$ is massive, showing an unnatural, instantly coordinated spike rather than a cascading spread), it calculates the **Arbitrage Index (AI)**. It uses **Network Assortativity** on the Crossref Event Data to distinguish between broadcast (PR) and peer-to-peer (Organic) dissemination.

### 3. Institutional Entropy Barometer (IEB)

**Description**
The Institutional Entropy Barometer (IEB) predicts catastrophic organizational failures—such as major lab safety incidents, massive grant clawbacks, or systemic research misconduct—up to 3 years before they become public. It operates on the premise that physical and regulatory decay in a research institution is preceded by subtle, measurable "entropy" in peripheral, publicly visible data streams. 

IEB correlates the frequency of OSHA violations or public safety reports with university ranking trajectories, but adds a crucial layer: the velocity of faculty migration (who is leaving and where are they going), the churn rate of post-docs (detected via ORCID employment history updates), and the complexity of NIH grant amendments. When an institution is failing internally, high-performing post-docs leave quietly, grant amendments requesting "change of scope" or "timeline extensions" spike, and co-authorship networks become intensely insular (researchers stop collaborating outside their failing department).

By tracking these vectors simultaneously, IEB creates a thermodynamic model of the institution. A high "entropy" score means the institutional scaffolding is collapsing. This tool allows federal agencies to audit labs preemptively and allows prospective PhD students to avoid joining toxic or collapsing departments.

**5+ Free Data Sources**

**Mathematical/Statistical Foundation**
IEB utilizes **Network Entropy (Shannon Entropy on Adjacency Matrices)** combined with **Cox Proportional-Hazards Models**. It builds a time-evolving co-authorship graph $G_t(V,E)$ for the institution. The Von Neumann entropy of the graph's normalized Laplacian matrix $L$ is calculated as $S(L) = -\sum \lambda_i \log \lambda_i$. A sudden drop in network entropy indicates the department is collapsing into isolated silos. This is fed into a Cox model along with covariates like safety violation rates and grant extension frequency to calculate the hazard ratio $h(t)$ of an impending institutional crisis.

### 4. Obfuscated Capital Nexus (OCN)

**Description**
The Obfuscated Capital Nexus (OCN) traces the "dark matter" of scientific funding—how shell companies, hostile state actors, or heavily conflicted corporate entities fund academic research through layered proxies. Often, a paper will declare funding from the "Institute for Dietary Research," which is actually a shell funded by a sugar conglomerate, or a foreign military contractor operating through a European holding company.

OCN unravels this by connecting the funding acknowledgment texts in academic papers to global corporate registries. It performs Named Entity Recognition (NER) on the raw text of the "Acknowledgements" and "Funding" sections. It then queries these entities against OpenCorporates to trace ultimate beneficial ownership. Simultaneously, it cross-references this with changes in patent assignees (via USPTO) and Foreign Agents Registration Act (FARA) databases. If a researcher declares funding from "Vanguard Tech Foundation," and OCN finds that Vanguard Tech shares a registered address with a sanctioned state entity, and the researcher's subsequent patents are assigned to a subsidiary of that entity, the nexus is exposed.

This tool calculates a "Conflict Obfuscation Score" for every paper. It reveals systemic industry capture of academic fields (e.g., vaping research, AI safety policy) by graphing the bipartite network of seemingly independent scientists and the hidden central nodes funding them.

### 5. Methodological Contagion Mapper (MCM)

**Description**
The Methodological Contagion Mapper (MCM) tracks how bad, misapplied, or outdated statistical methods spread through scientific literature like a virus. Often, a highly cited paper introduces a flawed methodology (e.g., a specific, inappropriate use of PCA or a flawed cell-line assay). Because the paper is highly cited, other researchers copy the methodology verbatim to ensure easy peer review, creating a "methodological monoculture" built on a flawed foundation.

MCM solves this by using NLP to extract the exact methodological steps from the "Materials and Methods" sections of millions of papers. It represents the methodology as an Abstract Syntax Tree (AST) of scientific operations. It then maps the citation network to see how this specific AST propagates. Crucially, it integrates data from PubPeer, Retraction Watch, and GitHub (for code implementations) to determine the "toxicity" of the method. 

If MCM detects that a deeply flawed statistical package version (e.g., an old Python library with a known bug, found via GitHub API) is being continually cited and replicated in biology papers, it maps the entire blast radius. It provides a "Contagion R0" value for the methodology, alerting journal editors when a submitted paper is utilizing a highly contagious, deprecated protocol.

**5+ Free Data Sources**
1. **OpenAlex API** (`api.openalex.org/works`): Citation graphs and N-grams.
2. **Europe PMC REST API**: Extracting the "Materials and Methods" text.
3. **GitHub Code Search API**: Finding specific usages of deprecated statistical libraries/functions mentioned in papers.
4. **Software Heritage Archive API** (`archive.softwareheritage.org/api`): Tracing the exact version and known bugs of the software used at the time of publication.
5. **Protocols.io API** (`www.protocols.io/api`): Matching the extracted methodology to community-corrected protocol forks.

**Mathematical/Statistical Foundation**
MCM relies on **Epidemiological Susceptible-Infected-Recovered (SIR) modeling on citation networks**, coupled with **Tree Edit Distance** for methodology AST comparison. The methodology of paper $A$ is converted to a tree $T_A$. If paper $B$ cites $A$, the methodology $T_B$ is compared using Zhang-Shasha edit distance. If $Distance(T_A, T_B) < \epsilon$, paper $B$ is "infected." The basic reproduction number $R_0$ of the methodology is calculated from the network topology. If $R_0 > 1$ and the method has a high bug/toxicity score from GitHub/Protocols.io, it is classified as a Critical Methodological Contagion.

### 6. Orphaned Knowledge Resurrector (OKR)

**Description**
The Orphaned Knowledge Resurrector (OKR) mines "failed" or forgotten research to solve contemporary problems. Thousands of chemical compounds, algorithmic approaches, or material science formulations are published, show marginal results for their *intended* use case at the time, and are abandoned (orphaned). Years later, a new field emerges where that exact discarded formulation is the perfect solution, but the fields don't share vocabulary, so the connection is never made.

OKR solves this by transforming the entire corpus of open-access scientific literature into a highly dimensional semantic vector space, specifically separating the *Properties* of an experiment from its *Application*. It fuses chemical/material databases with patent databases, historical arXiv dumps, and current clinical trial registries. When a researcher inputs a modern problem (e.g., "Need a polymer that resists degradation at 400C in a vacuum"), OKR doesn't search for modern aerospace papers. It searches the latent space for a 1985 paper on deep-sea drilling fluids that exhibited those exact physical properties as a *side-effect* but was abandoned because it was too expensive for drilling.

It mathematically bridges semantic drift across decades and disciplines, surfacing readymade, peer-reviewed solutions that save millions of dollars in redundant R&D.

**5+ Free Data Sources**
1. **PubChem REST API** (`pubchem.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/rest/pug`): Chemical properties and historical assays.
2. **Europe PMC Text-Mining API** (`www.ebi.ac.uk/europepmc/webservices/rest/search`): Extracting chemical/material annotations and physical properties from text.
3. **ClinicalTrials.gov API** (`clinicaltrials.gov/api/v2`): Finding abandoned trials due to lack of efficacy in one disease (to repurpose for another).
4. **USPTO Bulk Data / Patent API**: Identifying expired patents with specific unclaimed structural properties.
5. **DBpedia/Wikidata SPARQL Endpoint**: Mapping obsolete scientific terminology to modern ontologies (e.g., bridging 1970s material nomenclature to 2020s standards).

**Mathematical/Statistical Foundation**
OKR employs **Cross-Lingual/Cross-Temporal Word Embeddings (Dynamic Word2Vec)** mapped onto a **Bipartite Knowledge Graph**. Let $V_t$ be the vector space of scientific terms in decade $t$. OKR uses Procrustes alignment to map $V_{1980}$ to $V_{2020}$. It creates a matrix of Entity-Property $E$ and Entity-Application $A$. By calculating the cosine similarity in the Property space $cos(E_i, Target_{prop})$ while maximizing the distance in Application space $dist(A_i, Target_{app})$, it finds highly relevant materials used in totally alien contexts.

### 7. Nepotism Topology Engine (NTE)

**Description**
The Nepotism Topology Engine (NTE) algorithmically detects unstated, unethical peer-review rings, editorial nepotism, and academic cartel behavior. While basic COI checkers look for direct co-authorship within the last 3 years, academic cartels are much smarter. They use "bipartite rings" (Lab A reviews Lab B, Lab B reviews Lab C, Lab C reviews Lab A) or trade favorable reviews for future grant funding support.

NTE maps these invisible economies by fusing the OpenAlex citation graph, the Crossref peer-review metadata (when open), NIH/NSF study section rosters (who sat on the panel that funded who), and ProQuest Dissertations & Theses (identifying undisclosed PhD advisor/advisee relationships that persist decades later). 

It looks for "triadic closure anomalies" in the citation and funding networks. If Editor X accepts 40% of papers from Author Y, and Author Y heavily cites Author Z, and Author Z was Editor X's PhD advisor in 1998, NTE flags the cartel. It assigns a "Meritocratic Independence Score" to journals and labs, allowing universities to discount hiring candidates whose entire h-index is propped up by a 5-node cartel.

**5+ Free Data Sources**
1. **OpenAlex API**: Full co-authorship and citation graph.
2. **ProQuest Dissertations Database (via API/Web scraping or Crossref relationships)**: Establishing the immutable root advisor-advisee hierarchy.
3. **NIH Federal Advisory Committee Act (FACA) Database / Study Section Rosters**: Scraping public lists of who sat on which grant review panels.
4. **ORCID API**: Mapping employment history to detect "shared institutional lineage" (e.g., they didn't co-author, but they were post-docs in the same building in 2005).
5. **DOAJ (Directory of Open Access Journals) API**: Correlating editorial board memberships across multiple journals.

**Mathematical/Statistical Foundation**
NTE uses **Exponential Random Graph Models (ERGMs)** to calculate the probability of observed network ties given structural features. It specifically looks for **excessive network motifs** (like cyclic triangles or reciprocal dyads) that deviate from a random scale-free network expectation. The Nepotism Index $N$ is calculated using the algebraic connectivity (Fiedler value) of the subgraph. If a subset of researchers forms a strongly connected component with a high density $\rho$ but incredibly low betweenness centrality relative to the broader field, it is classified mathematically as an isolated cartel.

    if digits:
        first_digits = [int(str(d)[0]) for d in digits if d != 0]
        n = len(first_digits)
        if n > 0:
            counts = collections.Counter(first_digits)
            benford_expected = {d: math.log10(1 + 1/d) * n for d in range(1,10)}
            benford_dev = sum((counts[d] - benford_expected[d])**2 / benford_expected[d]

    word_freq = collections.Counter(words)
    ranks = list(range(1, min(100, len(word_freq))+1))
    freqs = [freq for _, freq in word_freq.most_common(len(ranks))]
    if len(ranks) > 1 and freqs[0] > 0:
